\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{System Model}

The system model used for the trajectory is based on the Frenet frame of reference [INSERT REFERENCE HERE]:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sys-eqs}
    \begin{aligned}
        s_{t+1} &= s_t + h \, v_{s,t} \\
        l_{t+1} &= l_t + h \, v_{l,t} \\
        v_{s,t+1} &= v_{s,t} + w_{s,t} \\
        v_{l,t+1} &= v_{l,t} + w_{l,t}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $s_t$ is the arc position, $h$ is the sampling period ($fs = 100Hz$), $l$ is the lateral deviation from the centre line of the track, $v_s$ is the arc velocity, $v_l$ is the lateral velocity, and $w_t$ is a gaussian noise term:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:noise}
    w_t \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\mathbf{0}, \begin{bmatrix} 0.04 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.0001 \end{bmatrix}\right)
\end{align}
which physically corresponds to small deviations in acceleration. The state vector is:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:state-vec}
    \mathbf{x_t} = \begin{bmatrix}s \\ l \\ v_s \\ v_l \end{bmatrix}, \quad
    A = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 & h & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & h \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
    G = \begin{bmatrix}0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
\end{align}
where $\mathbf{x}_{t}$ is the state vector, $A$ is the state transition matrix, $G$ is the noise transition matrix. Note that $G$ ensures noise is only introduced to the velocity compoents in the system. No uncertainty in position has been used in this model.

This state model is used to keep the dynamics of the system linear. By changing the co-ordinate basis of the state model, this shifts all the non-linearity into the measurement model of the system. As the problem is inherently non-linear, the Jacobian of the measurement model accounts for both the co-ordinate transformation and the range calculation.
\begin{align}
\label{eq:measurement-model}
    \mathbf{y}_t = ||r_{x, y} - r_{x, y}^{(i)}||_2 + o_t
\end{align}
where $\mathbf{y}$ is the output vector (Euclidean distance from each beacon), $r_{x, y}$ is the position of the vehicle on the track in cartesian co-ordinates, $r_{x, y}^{(i)}$ is the position of each beacon measurement in cartesian co-ordinates and $o_t$ is selected to be a Gaussian noise term. Noise is added independently to each beacon reading, with a standard deviation of 1.5m as defined in the specification.

There are defined physical limitations on the parameters of the vehicle as follows:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:state-constraints}
    \begin{aligned}
        l &\in [-2, 2] \\
        v_s &\in [0, 25] \\
        v_l &\in [-0.55, 0.55]
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The track is constructed by defining two circles of radii $r$ and $\rho$ centred at $(0, 0)$ and $(100, 0)$ respectively. Two tangent lines can be constructed based on the common tangent to both radii (external tangent) [INSERT REFERENCE HERE].

The outer boundary of the track can be defined by repeating this process for two additional circles of radii $R$ and $\rho + 4$m. The centreline of the track can then be generated simply by taking the average value of the inner and outer bounds.
To save on development time, the Claude Opus 4.6 model was used to construct the track geometry. The initial results of the model were incorrect, producing the internal tangent lines, and was adjusted to correct this. The function is included in code block~\ref{lst:track}.

\subsubsection{Co-ordinate transformation}

The base track geometry is defined in cartesian co-ordinates, and converted to the Frenet frame co-ordinates, $s, l$ where $s_i = f(x_i,y_i)$ is the arc position at index $i$, and $l$ is the deviation from the current arc position. $s$ is defined as follows:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:frenet-frame}
    s_i = \sum_{k=1}^{i} \sqrt{(x_{k} - x_{k-1})^2 + (y_{k} - y_{k-1})^2}
\end{equation}
Tangent and normal unit vectors are then pre-calculated for each point to allow conversion back to the cartesian co-ordinate system.

To convert from Frenet frame co-ordinates to the cartesian co-ordinate system, the closest point in the arc position $s$ is calculated via interpolation between $s$ and $(x,y)$ for each component. The unit vector can then be multiplied with $l$ as this term is just an offset from the centreline.

\subsubsection{Linearisation}

As the measurement model of the system is non-linear, an approximation method must be applied to the system to implement the Extended Kalman Filter estimator [INSERT REF TO PANTELIS' NOTEBOOKS HERE]. The Jacobian of a function is defined as follows:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:jacobian}
Jf(x) = \begin{bmatrix}
    \tfrac{\partial f_1(x)}{\partial x_1} & \tfrac{\partial f_1(x)}{\partial x_2} & \cdots & \tfrac{\partial f_1(x)}{\partial x_n}
    \\
    \vdots                                & \vdots                                & \ddots & \vdots
    \\
    \tfrac{\partial f_m(x)}{\partial x_1} & \tfrac{\partial f_m(x)}{\partial x_2} & \cdots & \tfrac{\partial f_m(x)}{\partial x_n}
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

Given that the measurement model is continuously differentiable, the Jacobian can be used to linearise via a first order Taylor approximation:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:linearisation}
    f(x) \approx f(x_0) + Jf(x_0) (x - x_0)
\end{equation}

This can be applied to each row of the beacon locations (3 distinct cartesian co-ordinate locations). Computation of the Jacobian was carried out using CasADi 3.7.2 [INSERT REFERENCE HERE].

\subsection{Beacon Placement}
\label{sec:beacon}
% Would be ideal to have pictures in here to highlight the different scenarios, don't think I have time though.
Location must be inferred from a minimum number of beacon measurements as outlined by the specification. A single beacon measurement is not sufficient to infer the position of the vehicle, as the Euclidean distance would only provide a radial distance from the singular beacon (the distance from the beacon could be anywhere on a circle surrounding the beacon).

Two beacons would present a localisation problem, as the location of the vehicle could be at either of two intersection points on the radial distances from the beacon positions.

Three beacons are the minimum number required to completely localise the position of the vehicle on the track geometry. Given this, the positioning of the beacons are considered to ensure that there is no bias to any one location on the track. As the beacon readings are available immediately and there is no increase in the additive noise variance to the system based on the range of the beacons, they can be placed further away to minimise the relative noise contribution.

The beacons are placed at the following positions in the track, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ekf-trajectory}:
\begin{equation}
    r_{x,y}^{3} = \begin{bmatrix} -100 & -60 \\ 20 & 90 \\ 180 & -10 \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Extended Kalman Filter}

The Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) has been used to estimate the state of the system. By applying the linearisation routine to the standard Kalman Filter system, an approximation of the measurement model can be formulated. In this system, the noise is additive, so $A$ is constant, $\tilde{Q}$ is constant, $\tilde{R}$ is constant and the only system matrix which changes is the measurement Jacobian $C = J_x h(\tilde{x})$
\footnote{
    We refer to Dr.\ Pantelis Sopasakis' full derivation from the following repository: \url{https://github.com/alphaville/EstimationTheoryNotebooks}
}.

For the EKF, the system must feature only gaussian noise terms, thus, the estimator cannot enforce constraints unless the assumptions of the estimator are violated. As the domain of the gaussian spans all $\mathbb{R}$, the lateral position of the vehicle will travel outside of the track. This is a known limitation of the EKF, in that constraints cannot be imposed. One method for forbidding unphysical behaviour in the system is to constrain the output of the estimated state to the only allowed domain of each respective state variable. This will also introduce an error in the measurement and time update terms, which would propagate through each subsequent update step, leading to an accumulation in error as the normality assumption has been violated. The estimated state trajectory would drift over time, causing the estimator to perform poorly.
The EKF is clearly not the correct approach for this type of problem, but provides a reasonable baseline approach to the problem.
